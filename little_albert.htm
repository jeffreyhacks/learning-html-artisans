The Little Albert Experiment

Ethical Controversies and the Foundations of Human Fear Conditioning

Historical Context

In 1920, behaviorist John B. Watson and his graduate student Rosalie Rayner conducted one of the most famous—and controversial—experiments in psychological history. Building directly on Pavlov's classical conditioning paradigm, Watson sought to demonstrate that emotional responses could be conditioned in humans, challenging the prevailing view that fears were innate or rooted in unconscious conflicts.

Key Objective: To determine whether a fear response could be artificially induced in a healthy infant through associative learning.

The subject, known to history as "Little Albert," was a nine-month-old infant from a hospital nursery. Watson selected Albert because he was described as emotionally stable and healthy. Prior to the experiment, Albert was exposed to various stimuli—a white rat, a rabbit, a dog, masks, and burning newspapers—showing no fear toward any of them.

The Conditioning Procedure

The experiment proceeded in carefully structured phases:

  • Baseline: Albert consistently showed no fear of the white rat, reaching for it without hesitation.
  • Acquisition: Watson and Rayner began pairing the rat (neutral stimulus) with a loud, jarring sound produced by striking a steel bar behind Albert's head (unconditioned stimulus).
  • Conditioned Response: After several pairings, Albert began to cry and crawl away at the mere sight of the rat—even when no sound was presented.
  • Stimulus Generalization: Crucially, Albert's fear extended to other furry objects: a rabbit, a dog, a sealskin coat, and even a Santa Claus mask.

"The instant the rat was shown, the baby began to cry. Almost instantly he turned sharply to the left, fell over on his left side, raised himself on all fours and began to crawl away so rapidly that he was caught with difficulty before reaching the edge of the table." — Watson & Rayner (1920)

Scientific Significance

The Little Albert experiment provided the first empirical evidence that human emotional responses could be conditioned through environmental associations. It demonstrated:

  • That fears need not be instinctive but could be learned.
  • The phenomenon of stimulus generalization, where fear transfers to similar objects.
  • A methodological bridge between animal and human conditioning research.

These findings laid crucial groundwork for later behavioral therapies, including systematic desensitization and exposure therapy, which help individuals unlearn maladaptive fears.

Ethical Considerations

By modern standards, the Little Albert experiment is considered deeply unethical.

  • No informed consent: Albert's mother was not fully informed of the procedure's nature.
  • Lack of debriefing: Albert left the hospital before any attempt was made to extinguish his conditioned fears.
  • Potential harm: The experiment deliberately induced distress in an infant with no therapeutic benefit.

These ethical failures contributed directly to the development of modern ethical guidelines, including the Belmont Report and institutional review boards (IRBs).

Key Figures

  • John B. Watson: Founder of behaviorism; argued for complete environmental determinism.
  • Rosalie Rayner: Co-researcher and later Watson's wife; her role was often minimized in historical accounts.
  • Ivan Pavlov: Provided the theoretical framework of classical conditioning that Watson applied.

Legacy and Unresolved Questions

The fate of Little Albert himself remained unknown for decades. Recent historical detective work by psychologist Hall Beck and others suggests that Albert may have been a child named Douglas Merritte, who died at age six of hydrocephalus—raising further questions about Watson's knowledge of Albert's health. Other researchers contest this identification, and the mystery endures.

Regardless of Albert's identity, his contribution to psychology—though involuntary—was profound. The experiment remains a staple in textbooks, a cautionary tale about research ethics, and a foundational demonstration of how environments shape the human mind.
