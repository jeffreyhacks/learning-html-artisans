Harlow's Monkey Experiments

Harry Harlow, Contact Comfort, and the Nature of Love

Historical Context

In the 1950s, psychology was dominated by behaviorist theories of attachment, which held that infants become attached to their mothers because mothers provide food—the "cupboard love" hypothesis [citation:3][citation:9]. Psychologist Harry Harlow at the University of Wisconsin-Madison suspected otherwise. Observing that his laboratory monkeys became strongly attached to the soft cloth diapers in their cages, he designed experiments to test whether comfort, not food, formed the basis of mother-infant bonding [citation:3][citation:6].

Core Question: Do infant monkeys prefer a mother who provides food or a mother who provides comfort and tactile contact?

The Surrogate Mother Experiments

Experiment 1: Wire vs. Cloth Mother

Harlow separated infant rhesus monkeys from their biological mothers within hours of birth and placed them in cages with two artificial "surrogate mothers" [citation:3][citation:9]:

  • Wire Mother: Made of welded wire mesh; cold and hard, but could be equipped with a feeding bottle.
  • Cloth Mother: Same wire frame but covered with soft terry cloth; warm and comforting, but no food.

For half the infants, the wire mother provided milk; for the other half, the cloth mother provided milk [citation:9].

Key Findings: Time Spent with Each Mother

Condition Wire Mother (hours/day) Cloth Mother (hours/day)
Wire mother feeds ~1 hour (feeding only) 17–18 hours (clinging, resting)
Cloth mother feeds ~1 hour 22–23 hours

Regardless of which mother provided food, infants overwhelmingly preferred the cloth mother. They would only go to the wire mother when hungry and immediately return to the cloth mother for comfort [citation:3][citation:9].

Fear Response and Secure Base

When a frightening object was placed in the cage, infants ran to the cloth mother for security. With the cloth mother present, they would eventually explore; without her, they cowered in fear [citation:6][citation:9].

Long-Term Effects of Deprivation

Isolation Experiments

Harlow later raised monkeys in total social isolation for varying periods (3, 6, 9, or 12 months). The results were devastating [citation:9]:

  • Isolated monkeys engaged in bizarre, repetitive behaviors: rocking, clutching themselves, self-mutilation.
  • When introduced to normal monkeys, they were terrified, then extremely aggressive.
  • They were unable to communicate or socialize and were bullied by others [citation:9].
  • Females became inadequate, abusive mothers, sometimes smashing their infants' faces [citation:9].

Critical Period

Monkeys isolated for less than 90 days could recover if placed in normal environments. Those isolated for longer than 90 days suffered permanent, irreversible damage [citation:9].

Core Concepts

  • Contact Comfort: The innate need for physical touch and comfort, independent of food [citation:6].
  • Secure Base: The mother provides a safe foundation for exploration.
  • Critical Period: A window for attachment formation; deprivation beyond this causes permanent harm.

Ethical Controversy

  • Harlow's experiments are now considered deeply unethical [citation:3][citation:9].
  • Monkeys suffered extreme psychological harm: depression, self-mutilation, social dysfunction.
  • Harlow himself controversially created an isolation chamber he called the "pit of despair."

Impact

  • Revolutionized attachment theory (influenced Bowlby).
  • Changed hospital and orphanage policies regarding parental contact.
  • Demonstrated that love and comfort are biological necessities.

Key Figures

  • Harry Harlow (1905–1981): American psychologist.
  • John Bowlby: Developed attachment theory based partly on Harlow's findings.

Conclusion: The Nature of Love

Harlow's experiments fundamentally transformed our understanding of attachment. As he famously concluded: "Love is a wondrous state, deep, tender, and rewarding. Because of its intimate and personal nature, it is regarded by some as an improper topic for experimental research. But whatever our personal feelings, we must put such questions to the test" [citation:3]. His work demonstrated that contact comfort is as essential as food—a finding that reshaped child-rearing practices, hospital protocols, and therapeutic approaches. Though ethically problematic by modern standards, the scientific contribution remains foundational [citation:3][citation:9].
