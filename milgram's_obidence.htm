The Milgram Obedience Experiment

Stanley Milgram and the Banality of Evil

Historical Context

In the early 1960s, the world was still grappling with the horrors of the Holocaust. The trial of Adolf Eichmann in Jerusalem had recently concluded, and the question echoed across intellectual circles: How could ordinary people participate in such atrocities? Psychologist Stanley Milgram, a Jewish American deeply influenced by these events, designed an experiment at Yale University to investigate a disturbing possibility—that ordinary individuals might commit harmful acts simply because they were following orders.

Core Question: Would ordinary citizens inflict pain on an innocent person when instructed by an authority figure, even when no direct coercion was involved?

Milgram recruited 40 male participants aged 20–50 through newspaper advertisements and direct mail, offering $4.50 for participation. They represented a cross-section of the population: postal clerks, engineers, teachers, and laborers. The experiment, they were told, was a study of memory and learning.

The Experimental Procedure

The setup was carefully orchestrated to create maximum psychological tension:

The Roles

  • The Experimenter: A stern, authoritative figure in a gray lab coat, played by a 31-year-old biology teacher. He gave prods when participants hesitated: "Please continue," "The experiment requires you to continue," "You have no choice, you must go on."
  • The Learner: A mild-mannered 47-year-old accountant, actually a confederate (Mr. Wallace). He was strapped to a chair with electrodes.
  • The Teacher: The real participant. He was given a sample shock (45V) to convince him the setup was real.

The Shock Machine

The participant was seated before an impressive shock generator with 30 lever switches, each labeled with voltage levels and verbal descriptors:

The learner was in another room, and the participant could hear his responses through the wall:

  • 75V: Grunts
  • 120V: Shouts that shocks are painful
  • 150V: Demands to be released
  • 270V: Agonized screams
  • 315V+: No response; ominous silence

If participants hesitated or wanted to stop, the experimenter delivered a sequence of prods, increasing in authority.

Results and Findings

Before the experiment, Milgram surveyed 14 Yale psychology seniors and psychiatrists. They predicted that only about 1–3% of participants would administer the maximum 450 volts, and that most would stop by 150 volts. The actual results shocked the world:

Baseline Experiment (Yale, 1961)

Voltage Level Percentage Stopping Cumulative Continuing
Up to 150V (first protest) 12.5% (5 participants) 87.5% continued
Up to 300V 22.5% (additional 9) 65% continued
Up to 450V (maximum) 65% (26 participants) 65% obeyed fully
"With numbing regularity, good people were seen to knuckle under the demands of authority and perform actions that were callous and severe... This is, perhaps, the most fundamental lesson of our study: ordinary people, simply doing their jobs, and without any particular hostility on their part, can become agents in a terrible destructive process." — Stanley Milgram

Participant Observations

Milgram documented intense psychological strain in participants:

  • Sweating, trembling, stuttering
  • Nervous laughter and fits of uncontrollable seizures
  • Three participants had full-blown, uncontrollable seizures
  • Many showed extreme tension but continued nonetheless

Ethical Firestorm

The experiment sparked decades of ethical debate:

  • Deception: Participants were told it was about memory, not obedience.
  • Psychological harm: Extreme stress, potential lasting trauma.
  • Right to withdraw: Prods ("you have no choice") undermined perceived freedom to quit.
  • Debriefing: Participants were reunited with the "learner" and assured no shocks occurred, but critics questioned whether this sufficed.

These controversies led directly to stricter ethical guidelines in psychological research, including the requirement for informed consent, debriefing, and institutional review boards (IRBs) .

Key Figures

  • Stanley Milgram (1933–1984): Student of Solomon Asch; later conducted the small-world experiment ("six degrees of separation").
  • Diana Baumrind: Early and influential critic of Milgram's ethics.

Legacy

The experiment remains a touchstone for understanding atrocities, organizational wrongdoing, and the power of situational forces over individual character.

Interpretations and Ongoing Debate

Milgram's findings have been interpreted through multiple lenses. The agentic state theory proposes that individuals shift into a state where they see themselves as instruments of another's will, relinquishing personal responsibility. Critics have questioned whether participants truly believed they were harming the learner; some post-experimental interviews suggested that many doubted the authenticity of the shocks, though subsequent replications with more realistic setups have found similar obedience rates.

Recent replications (Burger, 2009; Dolinski et al., 2017) have found obedience rates comparable to Milgram's, suggesting the phenomenon is robust. However, ethical constraints prevent full replication of Milgram's most stressful conditions. The experiment endures as a powerful warning about the destructive potential of ordinary people following authority, and as a cornerstone of social psychology.
